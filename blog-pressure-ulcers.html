Pressure Ulcers (Bed Sores): Prevention Tips for Seniors and Families

March 2026 8 min read Carolina Wound Care Team Aiken & Charleston, SC

If you're caring for an aging parent, spouse, or loved one who spends a lot of time in bed or a wheelchair, you may have noticed a red spot on the skin that just won't fade. That small red area could be the start of a pressure ulcer — also known as a bed sore.

It's completely understandable to feel worried. These wounds can develop quickly and cause pain, but here's the great news: most pressure ulcers can be prevented — and when they do appear, they heal well with the right care. At Carolina Wound Care, we help families across South Carolina every day protect their loved ones and get healing back on track.

What Is a Pressure Ulcer (Bed Sore)?

A pressure ulcer is an injury to the skin and underlying tissue caused by prolonged pressure. When someone can't change position easily, the pressure cuts off blood flow to certain areas — especially over bony spots like the tailbone, hips, heels, elbows, or shoulders.

Without fresh blood, the skin breaks down and an open wound can form. Common risk factors include:

  • Limited mobility after illness, surgery, or stroke
  • Older age and thinner, more fragile skin
  • Incontinence, poor nutrition, or dehydration
  • Diabetes or poor circulation

Good to Know

Pressure ulcers are often called "bed sores," but they can happen in wheelchairs too. The earlier they're caught, the easier they are to treat — and most can be prevented entirely with the right daily routine.

The Four Stages of Pressure Ulcers

Understanding the stages helps families recognize problems early and act quickly:

1
Stage 1 — Redness

Skin is red or darker and doesn't blanch (turn white) when pressed. The skin is still intact — this is the best time to act.

2
Stage 2 — Shallow Sore

Partial-thickness skin loss — looks like a shallow open sore or blister. Getting professional help now can prevent deeper damage.

3
Stage 3 — Deeper Wound

Full-thickness skin loss; a deeper crater that may show fat beneath. Specialist care is essential at this stage.

4
Stage 4 — Severe Wound

Very deep wound that may expose muscle, bone, or tendons. Requires immediate, expert specialist care.

Early Action Is Everything

Many ulcers are caught at Stage 1 and never progress when families know what to watch for and have expert support in place. If you spot redness that doesn't fade within 30 minutes of relieving pressure — call us.

Why Prevention and Early Care Matter So Much

Untreated pressure ulcers can lead to pain, infection, longer hospital stays, and reduced quality of life. For seniors and their families, they can also mean more stress and worry at an already difficult time.

The best part is that prevention works — and when an ulcer does develop, specialized care dramatically improves healing. Many of our patients' families tell us the biggest relief was simply having a clear plan and a caring team that comes directly to them.

When to Call Us Immediately

Contact Carolina Wound Care right away if you notice: a wound that is growing larger or deeper, signs of infection (fever, increased redness or warmth, foul odor, pus), or a Stage 3 or 4 wound. Early expert care makes a life-changing difference.

Simple Prevention Tips You Can Start Today

These everyday actions make a huge difference — and most of them cost nothing:

  • Reposition every 2 hours in bed (or every 15–30 minutes in a wheelchair)
  • Use pillows or foam wedges to keep pressure off bony areas
  • Keep skin clean and dry — especially after incontinence
  • Choose pressure-relieving mattresses, cushions, or heel protectors
  • Ensure good nutrition with plenty of protein, vitamins, and fluids
  • Check skin daily — use a mirror or flashlight for heels, the tailbone, and the back

The Two-Hour Rule

Repositioning every two hours is the single most effective thing a caregiver can do to prevent pressure ulcers in a bedridden loved one. If nighttime repositioning is difficult, ask us about pressure-redistribution mattresses — they can make a significant difference.
