What We Treat

Chronic Wounds That Need Specialist Care

A wound that hasn't improved in 2–4 weeks is considered chronic and deserves specialist evaluation. Early intervention dramatically improves outcomes.

Most Common High Risk

Diabetic Foot Ulcers

Diabetic foot ulcers affect approximately 15% of people with diabetes and are a leading cause of lower-limb amputation. They form due to a combination of peripheral neuropathy (loss of sensation), poor circulation, and impaired immune response — all common complications of diabetes.

Without expert wound care, these ulcers can progress rapidly, developing deep infections that reach bone (osteomyelitis) and potentially requiring amputation. With proper specialist intervention, the vast majority of diabetic foot ulcers can heal completely.

  • Specialized debridement to remove infected tissue
  • Offloading to remove pressure from the wound
  • Advanced biologics to stimulate healing
  • Infection management and prevention

Risk Factors

  • Diabetes (Type 1 or Type 2)
  • Peripheral neuropathy (numbness in feet)
  • Poor blood sugar control
  • Peripheral arterial disease
  • Foot deformities or pressure points
  • History of previous foot ulcers

Very Common Recurrent

Venous Leg Ulcers

Venous leg ulcers are caused by chronic venous insufficiency — when the veins in the legs can't efficiently return blood to the heart. This leads to fluid buildup, skin changes, and eventually open wounds that are notoriously difficult to heal without specialist intervention.

They typically appear above the ankle, may have irregular borders, and are often accompanied by swollen legs, brown skin discoloration, and a heavy, aching feeling. Many patients have lived with these wounds for months or even years before seeking specialist care.

  • Multi-layer compression bandaging therapy
  • Advanced moisture-retentive dressings
  • Edema management to address root cause
  • Recurrence prevention strategies

High Risk Limb-Threatening

Arterial (Ischemic) Ulcers

Arterial ulcers occur when reduced blood flow (ischemia) prevents adequate oxygen and nutrients from reaching the tissues. They are typically found on the toes, feet, or lower legs and are extremely painful — often worse at night when lying down.

These wounds are serious and can quickly become limb-threatening. They require immediate specialist evaluation and often a coordinated approach with vascular medicine to address the underlying circulation problem.

  • Careful, non-traumatic wound management
  • Coordinated vascular assessment and care
  • Pain management and positioning guidance
  • Infection prevention — critical for arterial wounds

Warning Signs

  • Severe pain, especially at night
  • Cold, pale or bluish toes/feet
  • Wounds on tips of toes or between toes
  • Hairless skin on lower legs
  • Weak or absent pulse in legs

Pressure Injuries / Bed Sores

Pressure injuries (also called pressure ulcers or bed sores) develop when sustained pressure cuts off blood flow to the skin and underlying tissue. They're common in patients with limited mobility — those who are bedridden, wheelchair-bound, or have had recent surgery or illness.

We treat all stages of pressure injuries, from Stage 1 redness to Stage 4 deep wounds involving bone. Our mobile home visit service is particularly important here — many patients with pressure wounds cannot travel to a clinic.

  • Staging, assessment, and wound bed preparation
  • Advanced debridement to remove necrotic tissue
  • Specialized dressings for each stage
  • Pressure relief guidance and repositioning plans
  • Caregiver and family education

Post-Op Complex

Post-Surgical Wounds

Not all surgical incisions heal as expected. Wound dehiscence (opening of the wound), infection, and non-healing after surgery are more common than most people realize — particularly in patients with diabetes, obesity, immunosuppression, or compromised circulation.

Our specialists work closely with your surgical team to manage these complex wounds using advanced techniques that standard home health cannot provide — including debridement, biologics, and UltraMist® therapy to accelerate closure.

  • Wound dehiscence management
  • Surgical site infection treatment
  • Abdominal, chest, and orthopedic wound care
  • Ostomy and fistula management
Schedule Consultation

Signs Your Surgical Wound Needs Specialist Care

  • Wound edges separating (dehiscence)
  • Increasing redness, warmth, or swelling
  • Drainage that is cloudy, green, or foul-smelling
  • No sign of healing after 2–3 weeks
  • Fever associated with the wound site
  • Deep cavities or tunneling developing

Who Is Most at Risk

  • Elderly patients with limited mobility
  • Nursing home or long-term care residents
  • Patients recovering from hip fracture or surgery
  • Those with incontinence (moisture damage)
  • Patients with poor nutrition or low body weight

Sacral Ulcers

Sacral ulcers are pressure injuries located over the sacrum (lower back / tailbone area). They are among the most common and most serious types of pressure wounds, particularly in elderly, bedridden, or incontinent patients.

Because of their location, sacral ulcers are particularly difficult to treat — they're constantly at risk of contamination and additional pressure. Our mobile team specializes in visiting nursing homes and private homes to provide advanced sacral wound care without patients needing to be moved to a clinic.

  • Expert staging and wound bed preparation
  • Advanced moisture-barrier and antimicrobial dressings
  • Debridement and biologic application as needed
  • Nursing facility staff education and support

Is This Right For You?

Is Carolina Wound Care Right for You?

Here's a clear guide to help you know whether our specialized services are the right fit for your situation.

This IS for you if…

  • Your wound has not improved in 2–6 weeks
  • You have diabetes and a wound on your foot or leg
  • You've been told you may need an amputation
  • You have a pressure wound / bed sore (any stage)
  • Your surgical wound hasn't closed properly
  • You have a venous or arterial leg ulcer
  • You struggle to get to a clinic (mobility / transport)
  • You want specialist-level care at home
  • You have Medicare or a PPO plan
  • You live anywhere in South Carolina

This is NOT for you if…

  • You have a minor fresh cut or scrape healing normally
  • You need emergency/acute trauma care (go to ER)
  • Your wound is actively and visibly improving weekly
  • You need inpatient hospital care or surgery
