The 4 Stages of Wound Healing – And What Happens When Healing Gets Stuck

March 2026 8 min read Carolina Wound Care Team Aiken & Charleston, SC

When a wound forms, your body immediately gets to work. It knows exactly what to do — and it follows a precise, four-stage process to close and repair the damage. Most of the time, this process runs beautifully on its own. But sometimes it gets interrupted — and that's when a wound becomes chronic.

Understanding these four stages can help you recognize what's happening with your wound, feel more confident about your care, and know when it's time to call for professional help. At Carolina Wound Care, we guide patients through every stage — in our clinics in Aiken and Charleston, and with mobile visits anywhere in South Carolina.

The 4 Natural Stages of Wound Healing

Every wound — whether it's a surgical incision, a diabetic foot ulcer, or a pressure sore — follows the same basic healing path. Here's what each stage looks like:

1

Hemostasis — Stopping the Bleeding

What happens: The moment a wound occurs, your body's first priority is to stop the bleeding. Blood vessels narrow, platelets rush to the site and clump together, and a clot forms to seal the wound. This stage happens within minutes to hours and is your body's emergency response — fast and automatic.

What you see: Bleeding slowing and stopping; the beginning of a scab or dried clot forming over the wound.

2

Inflammation — Cleaning and Defending

What happens: Once bleeding is controlled, the immune system moves in. White blood cells flood the wound to destroy bacteria and clear away damaged tissue. Blood vessels widen slightly to allow more immune cells in. This stage typically lasts three to five days for a normal wound.

What you see: Redness, warmth, swelling, and tenderness around the wound — these are signs the immune system is doing its job, not signs that something is wrong. Some clear or light yellow drainage is also normal.

3

Proliferation — Rebuilding Tissue

What happens: This is where the real repair begins. The body builds new tissue, collagen, and blood vessels to fill and close the wound. New skin cells migrate across the wound surface from the edges inward. This stage can last anywhere from a few weeks to a month depending on the wound's size and depth.

What you see: The wound filling in from the bottom up; new pink or red tissue forming; the wound edges gradually closing together.

4

Remodeling — Strengthening the Repair

What happens: After the wound closes, the body continues working underneath the surface. Collagen fibers reorganize and strengthen. The scar tissue gradually becomes more durable and flexible. This final stage can last up to two years in some cases — long after the wound appears healed on the outside.

What you see: The wound closes and a scar forms. Over time the scar may fade and flatten. The repaired area may feel itchy or tight as collagen reorganizes — this is normal.

Understanding the Timeline

A simple cut may move through all four stages in one to two weeks. A deeper or more complex wound — especially in someone with diabetes, poor circulation, or other health challenges — may take weeks to months. What matters most is that forward progress keeps happening. A wound that's stuck in one stage for more than a few weeks needs professional attention.

When Healing Gets Stuck — What Causes Chronic Wounds?

Normally the four stages flow smoothly from one to the next. But sometimes something interrupts the process — and the wound gets "stuck," unable to progress to the next stage. This is called a chronic wound.

Here are the most common reasons healing stalls:

  • Poor blood flow (poor circulation) — Blood carries oxygen and nutrients to the wound site. When circulation is reduced — due to peripheral artery disease, venous insufficiency, or other conditions — the wound doesn't get the fuel it needs to heal.
  • Diabetes — High blood sugar damages nerves and blood vessels, slowing the delivery of healing resources and impairing the immune response at the wound site.
  • Continued pressure — For pressure ulcers, sitting or lying on the same area repeatedly compresses the skin and cuts off blood supply before new tissue can form.
  • Swelling (edema) — Excess fluid in the tissue around a wound creates a barrier that blocks oxygen and nutrients from reaching the healing cells. Venous leg ulcers are a common example.
  • Poor nutrition — Without adequate protein, Vitamin C, zinc, and calories, the body simply doesn't have the raw materials to build new tissue and collagen.
  • Infection — Bacteria in or around the wound trigger a sustained inflammatory response that prevents the body from moving forward to the rebuilding stage.
  • Age and immune challenges — Older adults and people with weakened immune systems may have a reduced healing response overall, slowing each stage of the process.

When to Get Professional Help

If a wound hasn't shown clear signs of improvement — less drainage, smaller size, closing edges — within 2 to 4 weeks, it's time to see a specialist. Waiting longer allows the wound to worsen and may significantly increase the risk of infection or other complications. Don't wait — early care makes a real difference.

There Is Real Hope — And Real Help

Hearing that your wound is "stuck" can feel discouraging. But here's what we want you to know: a stalled wound is not a failed wound. With the right professional care, most chronic wounds can be restarted through the healing process — even if they've been present for months.

Advanced wound care specialists like the team at Carolina Wound Care are trained specifically to identify exactly where in the healing process a wound has stalled and why — and then apply targeted treatments to get things moving again. Simple Steps You Can Take to Support Healing

While professional care is essential for chronic wounds, there's a lot you can do every day to support your body's natural healing process:

  • Keep the wound clean and covered — Follow your wound care provider's dressing instructions carefully. A clean, moist wound environment supports tissue growth.
  • Eat nutrient-rich foods — Protein, Vitamin C, zinc, and good hydration are the building blocks of tissue repair. See our Nutrition for Wound Healing guide for easy, practical tips.
  • Stay as active as safely possible — Movement improves circulation, which delivers oxygen and nutrients to the wound site. Even short, gentle walks help.
  • Avoid pressure on the wound — If you have a pressure ulcer, reposition every 2 hours (or every 15–30 minutes in a chair). Use cushions and pressure-relieving mattresses where possible.
  • Track any changes — Check the wound daily in good light. Increased redness, swelling, warmth, more drainage, or fever can all be early signs of infection — and early action matters.

Small Habits, Big Impact

The most powerful thing you can do between professional visits is stay consistent with the basics: clean dressings, good nutrition, daily checks, and staying active. These simple habits keep the healing environment stable and give your body the best possible chance to move forward through each stage.
