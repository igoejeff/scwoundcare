Condition 1

Diabetic Foot Ulcers

Diabetic foot ulcers are open sores or wounds that occur on the feet of people with diabetes, most commonly on the bottom of the foot. They affect approximately 15% of people with diabetes at some point in their lives and are a leading cause of hospitalization and lower-limb amputation.

According to the American Diabetes Association: Specialized wound care reduces the risk of amputation by up to 85% when initiated early. Early intervention is the single most important factor in saving limbs and lives.

How We Treat Diabetic Foot Ulcers

  • Regular debridement to remove necrotic tissue and stimulate healing
  • Offloading — removing pressure from the wound site
  • Advanced wound dressings for moisture balance and infection control
  • Biologic grafts for wounds not responding to standard care
  • Blood sugar coordination with your endocrinologist/PCP
Read our guide to diabetic foot ulcers

Warning Signs — When to Call

If you or a loved one has diabetes and notices any of the following, contact us immediately:

  • A sore, blister, or wound on the foot that isn't healing
  • Redness, swelling, warmth, or odor around a wound
  • Dark, discolored, or black tissue around a wound
  • A wound that has been present for more than 2 weeks

Condition 2

Venous Leg Ulcers

Venous leg ulcers are caused by inadequate blood flow from the legs back to the heart, leading to fluid buildup, skin breakdown, and open wounds — typically on the lower leg near the ankle. They are the most common type of leg ulcer and have a high recurrence rate without proper management.

Clinical Note: Compression therapy combined with specialist wound care is the gold standard for venous ulcers. Studies show this combination reduces healing time by 50% or more compared to wound care alone.

Our Venous Ulcer Protocol

  • Multi-layer compression bandaging to reduce edema
  • Wound bed preparation and regular debridement
  • Moisture-balancing wound dressings
  • Patient education on compression stocking maintenance
  • Recurrence prevention planning

Common Symptoms

  • Shallow, irregularly shaped wound near the ankle
  • Leg swelling, especially worse after standing
  • Brown skin discoloration near the wound
  • Itching, burning, or aching pain in the lower leg
  • Wound with yellow or fibrous tissue on the surface

Condition 3

Pressure Injuries (Bedsores)

Pressure injuries — also called bedsores, pressure ulcers, or decubitus ulcers — are areas of damaged skin and tissue caused by sustained pressure, typically over bony prominences like heels, hips, tailbone, and elbows. They are particularly common in immobile patients in nursing homes, hospitals, and home care settings.

According to the National Pressure Injury Advisory Panel (NPIAP): Pressure injuries affect approximately 2.5 million patients annually in the US and are associated with significant pain, infection risk, and mortality.

Pressure Injury Stages

  • Stage 1:Non-blanchable redness — skin intact
  • Stage 2:Partial thickness skin loss — open wound or blister
  • Stage 3:Full thickness skin loss — visible fat tissue
  • Stage 4:Full thickness tissue loss — exposed bone or tendon

We Treat All Stages

Our specialists are experienced with pressure injuries at every stage, including deep tissue injuries and unstageable wounds.

  • In-home visits to nursing facilities and assisted living
  • Debridement and infection management
  • Specialty wound dressings for pressure injuries
  • Nutritional assessment and recommendations
  • Repositioning education for caregivers and facility staff

Additional Wound Types We Treat

Arterial / Ischemic Wounds

Caused by insufficient blood flow to the extremities due to arterial disease. These wounds appear on the lower legs and feet, are often painful, and require prompt intervention to prevent tissue loss.

Expert tip: Arterial wounds should always be evaluated by a specialist — inadequate treatment can lead to serious complications including limb loss.

Post-Surgical Wounds

Surgical incisions that fail to close properly, dehisce (open), or become infected require specialized wound care beyond what a primary care provider can typically offer. Our team manages complex post-surgical wounds from any procedure.

Read our post-surgical wound care guide

Sacral Wounds

The sacrum (tailbone area) is one of the most common sites for pressure injuries in bedridden patients. Sacral wounds can deteriorate rapidly and require expert management, especially in nursing home or homebound settings.

Traumatic Wounds

Wounds resulting from trauma, skin tears, lacerations, or road rash that aren't healing properly. Our team provides advanced care to minimize scarring and ensure full closure.

Burns & Radiation Wounds

Partial-thickness burns and radiation-induced skin damage benefit significantly from specialized wound care and advanced dressings that promote healing while managing pain.

Lymphedema-Related Wounds

Chronic swelling from lymphedema can cause skin breakdown and wound formation. We provide compression therapy and wound care to manage these complex, recurring wounds.

Is This Right for You?

We Can Help If You…

We Can Help If You…

  • Have a wound that hasn't healed in 2 or more weeks
  • Have a diabetic foot ulcer or venous leg ulcer
  • Have a pressure wound, bedsore, or sacral wound
  • Have a post-surgical wound that isn't healing properly
  • Have limited mobility and find clinic visits difficult
  • Reside in or near a skilled nursing or assisted living facility
  • Want a board-certified wound specialist — not just a general practitioner
  • Are in South Carolina and need in-home or clinic-based care

We May Not Be the Right Fit If…

  • Your wound is minor and actively healing on its own
  • You require emergency surgical intervention (please go to the ER)
  • You need care exclusively covered by a different specialist type
  • You are located outside of our South Carolina service area
  • Your wound requires inpatient hospital-level management only
