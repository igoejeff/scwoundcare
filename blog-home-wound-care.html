How to Care for Your Wound at Home Between Professional Visits

March 2026 7 min read Carolina Wound Care Team Aiken & Charleston, SC

Healing a chronic wound takes a team effort — and you're a vital part of that team. Your professional visits with Carolina Wound Care provide the expert assessment, specialized treatments, and clinical oversight that drive real progress. But the hours between those visits? That's where what you do at home can either support your healing or slow it down.

The good news: home wound care doesn't need to be complicated. With a few simple habits and some guidance from your care team, you can keep your wound on the right track every single day. This guide walks you through what to do — and when to call us right away.

Important Reminder

Home care supports your professional treatment — it does not replace it. Never change your wound care routine without checking with your Carolina Wound Care specialist first. Your personalized care plan is designed specifically for your wound and your health.

Why Home Care Matters

Think of your wound like a project in progress. Your specialist provides the skilled work — debridement, specialized dressings, therapeutic treatments — but between visits, your wound still needs attention. Even small daily habits make a meaningful difference in how quickly and safely you heal.

Research consistently shows that patients who follow their home care instructions between clinic visits heal significantly faster than those who don't. That's not about pressure — it's about possibility. You have real power to support your own healing, and we want you to feel confident using it.

Daily Home Care Steps

Follow these steps every day as directed by your Carolina Wound Care team. If your care plan includes specific instructions that differ from what's listed here, always follow your personalized plan first.

1

Wash Your Hands First — Every Time

Before touching your wound or any supplies, wash your hands thoroughly with soap and warm water for at least 20 seconds. This single step prevents the vast majority of wound infections. Never skip it, even if you're "just checking." Clean gloves are even better if available.

2

Gentle Wound Cleaning

Unless your care team has instructed otherwise, gently rinse your wound with clean lukewarm water or saline. Avoid hydrogen peroxide, iodine, or alcohol-based products — these can damage the delicate new tissue that's trying to form. Pat the area dry with a clean, soft cloth or gauze; never rub.

3

Dressing Changes as Instructed

Follow your care team's instructions exactly — including the type of dressing, how often to change it, and how to secure it. Keep the wound covered at all times unless your specialist specifically tells you otherwise. A moist, covered wound environment supports faster healing and reduces infection risk.

4

Protect the Wound & Relieve Pressure

Pressure on a healing wound — whether from sitting, lying, or standing — is one of the most common reasons wounds stall. Use cushions, heel protectors, or positioning aids as recommended. If you have a diabetic foot ulcer or a pressure ulcer, off-loading that area is non-negotiable. Every hour of unnecessary pressure undoes progress.

5

Daily Monitoring

Look at your wound every day (use a mirror if needed for hard-to-see areas). Note the wound's size, color, drainage amount and color, and any new symptoms. Tracking daily changes gives you and your care team valuable information — and helps you catch problems early before they escalate.

Other Helpful Habits That Support Healing

Wound healing doesn't happen in isolation — your whole body is involved. These everyday habits give your wound the best possible environment to heal:

Eat for Healing

Protein, vitamin C, zinc, and vitamin A are the cornerstones of wound healing. Focus on lean meats, eggs, beans, leafy greens, citrus, and nuts. Even small improvements in nutrition can noticeably speed up progress.

Stay Gently Active

Light movement improves blood flow to your wound — the oxygen and nutrients carried in your blood are the raw materials healing requires. Short, gentle walks or light chair exercises (as approved by your doctor) keep circulation going without overloading the wound site.

Stay Hydrated

Dehydration slows every repair process in the body, including wound healing. Aim for 6–8 glasses of water per day unless your doctor has recommended otherwise. Herbal teas and diluted fruit juice count too.

Manage Blood Sugar & Health

If you have diabetes, keeping your blood glucose in your target range is one of the most powerful things you can do. High blood sugar directly impairs the healing process. Take your medications, check your levels, and share readings with your care team at every visit.

Elevate When Resting

If you have a wound on your leg or foot, elevating it above heart level while you rest helps reduce swelling and improve circulation. Swelling keeps oxygen and nutrients from reaching the wound — reducing it is a simple, free way to support healing every single day.

When to Call Us Right Away

Home care is powerful, but there are clear signs that mean your wound needs professional attention now — don't wait for your next scheduled visit. Call Carolina Wound Care at 866-540-8090 immediately if you notice:

  • Increasing pain — wound that hurts more than usual or pain that woke you up
  • Spreading redness around the wound edges or red streaks extending outward
  • Increased or changed drainage — more fluid than normal, or drainage that turns yellow, green, or has a foul odor
  • New swelling or warmth around the wound site
  • Fever above 100.4°F (38°C) or chills, especially if they come on suddenly
  • Wound growing larger or clearly not improving despite careful home care
  • Any sign of deep damage — exposed bone, tendon, or tissue that looks significantly different

Don't Wait — Call Right Away

If you notice any of the warning signs above, call us immediately at 866-540-8090. Early action on a potential infection or complication is always better than waiting. Simple Tips to Make Home Care Easier

  • Set a daily reminder on your phone for dressing changes and wound checks so they become a consistent habit
  • Keep a dedicated wound supply basket near where you typically do your dressing changes — having everything in one place makes the process much less stressful
  • Involve a trusted family member or caregiver — having someone help with hard-to-reach areas makes wound care safer and more thorough
  • Keep a small wound journal — note the date, what you see, and any changes or concerns. Bring it to every visit. It's one of the most useful tools we have
  • Schedule visits around your routine — you can often coordinate clinic visits or mobile visits at times that don't disrupt your day
  • Call us with questions — there's no such thing as a silly question about your wound. Our team is always happy to clarify your care plan
