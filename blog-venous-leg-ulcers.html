Venous Leg Ulcers: Causes, Symptoms, and the Care That Actually Works

March 2026 8 min read Carolina Wound Care Team Aiken & Charleston, SC

If you have a sore on your lower leg or ankle that just won't close — especially one that leaks, itches, or feels heavy — you may be dealing with a venous leg ulcer. It can feel discouraging and even a little scary, but here's the truth: these ulcers are very treatable, and most people heal well with the right care.

At Carolina Wound Care, we see patients from Aiken, Charleston, and all across South Carolina who thought they would have to live with this problem forever. Once they start with us, they often say the same thing: "I wish I had come sooner." You don't have to wait. Understanding what's happening and getting expert help can change everything.

What Is a Venous Leg Ulcer?

A venous leg ulcer is an open wound that usually forms on the lower leg or around the ankle. It happens when the veins in your legs have trouble sending blood back up to your heart — a condition called venous insufficiency. Blood pools in the lower legs, causing swelling and pressure that eventually damages the skin and creates an ulcer.

Common reasons this develops include:

  • A history of blood clots (DVT)
  • Varicose veins
  • Prolonged standing or sitting
  • Previous leg injuries or surgeries
  • Obesity, pregnancy, or family history

Did You Know?

Venous leg ulcers are one of the most common types of chronic wounds, especially in adults over 65. Without proper care, they can last for months or even years — but with specialized attention, most heal completely.

Common Signs and Symptoms

You might notice these changes before the ulcer even opens:

  • Swelling in the lower leg or ankle (especially at the end of the day)
  • Aching, heaviness, or cramping in the legs
  • Brownish or reddish skin discoloration (called "stasis dermatitis")
  • Itchy, dry, or scaly skin
  • Varicose veins or hardened skin around the ankle
  • An open sore that drains clear or yellowish fluid and may have a strong odor

Most venous ulcers appear on the inner ankle, but they can show up anywhere on the lower leg. The skin around the wound often feels warm or tender. If you recognize any of these signs, don't wait for it to get worse — earlier care means faster, more complete healing.

Why Early Care Makes Such a Big Difference

Left untreated, venous leg ulcers can lead to infection, larger wounds, and ongoing pain that limits walking and daily life. The good news is that once the underlying circulation issue is addressed through expert wound care, healing usually begins quickly — and the chance of the ulcer coming back drops dramatically.

Many patients tell us they tried everything at home — creams, wraps, elevation — but nothing worked until they had a consistent, professional plan. That's exactly what we provide at Carolina Wound Care.

Don't Wait Too Long

A venous leg ulcer that is left untreated can become infected and enlarge significantly. If you notice increasing redness, warmth, swelling, fever, or a foul odor from a wound, seek professional care promptly.

Simple Steps You Can Take Today

While professional care is the key to real healing, these daily habits make a meaningful difference between visits:

  • Elevate your legs above heart level for 30 minutes, several times a day
  • Move around regularly (short walks or ankle pumps if you can't walk far)
  • Wear prescribed compression stockings if your doctor recommends them
  • Keep the skin clean and well-moisturized
  • Avoid crossing your legs for long periods

Small Steps, Big Results

Elevation and gentle movement are two of the most powerful things you can do between specialist visits. Even 20–30 minutes of elevation a few times a day can reduce the swelling that drives venous leg ulcers — and noticeably speed up healing.
