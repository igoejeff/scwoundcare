The 4 Stages of Wound Healing

Normal wound healing follows a predictable sequence of four overlapping phases. Chronic wounds get "stuck" in one of these phases — which is why specialist intervention is required to restart the healing process.

1 Stage

Hemostasis (Stopping the Bleeding)

Begins within seconds of injury

The moment an injury occurs, the body immediately activates its clotting mechanism to stop bleeding. Blood vessels constrict, and platelets rush to the wound site to form a clot — creating a temporary seal over the wound.

This platelet plug also releases chemical signals (growth factors) that trigger the next healing phases. The clot that forms becomes the scaffolding upon which new tissue will eventually grow.

What's happening in your wound

  • Blood vessels constrict to reduce blood loss
  • Platelets aggregate and form a clot
  • Growth factors are released to signal healing
  • A fibrin matrix (scab foundation) forms
2 Stage

Inflammation (Cleaning the Wound)

Days 1–4 after injury

Inflammation is not a problem — it's a necessary and purposeful healing phase. White blood cells (neutrophils and macrophages) flood the wound to kill bacteria, remove debris, and digest dead tissue. This is why wounds appear red, warm, and swollen.

In chronic wounds, this phase becomes dysregulated — the inflammatory response continues indefinitely without progressing to repair. This "chronic inflammation" actively destroys healing tissue instead of building it. This is why specialist debridement is often needed to break the cycle.

Signs of this stage

  • Redness and warmth around the wound
  • Swelling (edema)
  • Clear or yellowish wound drainage
  • Some pain or tenderness

Chronic wounds get stuck here. Specialist care helps break the inflammatory cycle and move healing forward.

3 Stage

Proliferation (Building New Tissue)

Days 4–21 after injury

This is the rebuilding phase. New blood vessels form (angiogenesis), and fibroblasts lay down collagen to fill the wound with new tissue (granulation tissue). The wound begins to visibly shrink as new tissue fills the defect from the bottom up.

Epithelial cells migrate from the wound edges across the new tissue surface to gradually close the wound. This phase requires adequate nutrition, blood supply, and a clean wound bed — all things our specialists work to optimize.

What you'll see

  • Red, bumpy granulation tissue filling the wound
  • Wound becoming visibly smaller
  • Reduced drainage
  • New pink skin appearing at wound edges
4 Stage

Remodeling (Strengthening the Scar)

Weeks 3 through 2+ years

After the wound closes, the body continues to remodel the scar tissue for months to years. Collagen fibers reorganize, and the scar tissue gradually gains strength — though it will only ever reach about 80% of the strength of original skin.

During this phase, it's important to protect the healing area, manage any recurrence risk factors (especially for diabetic or venous ulcers), and follow your care plan for prevention of re-injury.

Long-term healing tips

  • Protect healed skin from pressure and injury
  • Maintain compression therapy for venous ulcers
  • Continue foot care for diabetic patients
  • Follow up with your wound care specialist
Healing Support

Nutrition & Lifestyle for Faster Healing

Wound healing requires significant nutritional resources. What you eat and how you live directly impacts how quickly and completely your wound heals.

Protein is Essential

Protein provides the amino acids needed to build collagen and new tissue. Aim for 1.2–1.5g of protein per kg of body weight daily. Good sources: chicken, fish, eggs, Greek yogurt, legumes.

Vitamin C

Vitamin C is critical for collagen synthesis and immune function. Low vitamin C significantly impairs healing. Include citrus fruits, bell peppers, strawberries, and broccoli daily.

Stay Well Hydrated

Dehydration impairs every stage of wound healing. Aim for at least 8–10 glasses of water daily. Adequate hydration also prevents pressure injury development in bedridden patients.

Quit Smoking

Smoking dramatically impairs wound healing — reducing blood flow to the skin by up to 50%, reducing tissue oxygen delivery, and suppressing immune function. Quitting is one of the most impactful things you can do.

Blood Sugar Control

For diabetic patients, maintaining blood sugar in target range is critical. High blood glucose impairs white blood cell function, reduces circulation, and dramatically slows all phases of wound healing.

Movement & Positioning

For venous ulcers, regular walking and leg elevation improves blood return. For pressure wounds, regular repositioning (every 2 hours) is essential. Ask your care team for specific guidance.

Terminology

Wound Care Glossary

Understanding common wound care terms helps you be a more informed participant in your care.

Debridement The removal of dead, damaged, or infected tissue from a wound to promote healing and reduce infection risk.
Granulation Tissue The new, pink, moist tissue that fills a healing wound. It has a bumpy appearance and is a sign of healthy healing progress.
Slough Yellow or white moist dead tissue in a wound that must be removed (debrided) to allow healing to progress.
Eschar Hard, dark (brown or black) necrotic (dead) tissue that must typically be removed for a wound to heal.
Undermining / Tunneling Areas of tissue destruction that extend under the skin edges or through tissue in specific directions, indicating a deeper wound than visible.
Exudate Fluid that seeps from a wound (wound drainage). The amount, color, and consistency provides important information about healing progress and infection.
Periwound The skin surrounding the wound. Periwound condition (maceration, excoriation, redness) is an important part of wound assessment.
Biofilm A community of bacteria encased in a protective matrix that is highly resistant to antibiotics. Biofilm is a major cause of chronic wound chronicity and must be disrupted through debridement.
Neuropathy Nerve damage — particularly relevant in diabetes — causing loss of sensation in the feet. Patients cannot feel injuries, leading to unnoticed wounds that develop into serious ulcers.

Common Questions About Wound Healing

Most acute wounds (cuts, minor surgical wounds) heal within 2–4 weeks. If a wound has not shown clear signs of improvement — getting measurably smaller, with less drainage and decreasing redness — within 2–4 weeks, it should be evaluated by a wound care specialist. Wounds not healed by 6 weeks are definitively classified as chronic and require specialist intervention.
Signs of wound infection include: increasing redness, warmth, and swelling (beyond 48–72 hours post-injury); purulent (cloudy, yellow, or green) drainage; foul odor; increasing pain; fever; and red streaking extending from the wound. If you notice these signs, seek medical attention promptly. Call us at 866-540-8090 and we can assess the situation quickly.
Modern wound care science has firmly established that moist wound healing leads to significantly better and faster outcomes than dry healing. Keeping a wound properly moist (not wet or waterlogged) maintains the optimal environment for cell migration, reduces pain, prevents scarring, and accelerates healing. This is why advanced dressings significantly outperform dry gauze.

Vitamins A, C, and zinc are also critical. Poor nutrition can mean a wound that could heal in 8 weeks instead takes 6 months — or doesn't heal at all. Our specialists always assess nutritional status as part of your care plan.
