Appointments & Scheduling

How to get started and what to expect

In most cases, no referral is required. You can call us directly at 866-540-8090 or request an appointment online. We accept self-referrals from patients.

Some insurance plans may require a referral from your primary care physician — if yours does, we can help you navigate that process when you call. It's usually a simple step that we can assist with.

We prioritize fast access to care because wound care delays can lead to serious complications. In most cases, we can schedule:

  • In-clinic visits in Aiken or Charleston: same week, often within 1–3 days
  • Mobile home visits: typically within 2–5 days of your call

If your wound is urgent, tell us when you call — we will do our best to accommodate you as quickly as possible. Call 866-540-8090.

For your first visit, please bring:

  • Your insurance card(s) and photo ID
  • A list of all current medications and dosages
  • Any recent lab results (HbA1c, blood work, etc.)
  • Previous wound care records or photos, if available
  • Name and contact info for your primary care physician
  • A list of your medical conditions (diabetes, heart disease, etc.)

If this is a home visit, simply have these items available when our specialist arrives.

Your first visit typically includes:

  • Comprehensive wound assessment — measurements, photographs, tissue type analysis
  • Full medical history review — relevant conditions, medications, prior treatments
  • Vascular and circulation assessment if relevant to your wound type
  • Insurance verification review and cost discussion
  • Personalized care plan — specific treatments recommended, expected frequency, goals
  • Initial treatment may be performed at the first visit depending on the wound

We take our time at the first appointment — typically 45–60 minutes — so nothing is rushed and all your questions are answered.

Visit frequency depends on your wound type, severity, and healing response. A typical schedule looks like:

  • Initial phase: 1–3 visits per week while the wound is actively being treated
  • Healing phase: Weekly visits as the wound improves and reduces in size
  • Maintenance phase: Every 1–2 weeks as the wound approaches closure

Your care plan will outline the expected visit frequency and we adjust it based on how your wound responds. Many patients see significant improvement within 2–4 weeks of starting care.

Simply call us at 866-540-8090 as soon as possible if you need to reschedule. We ask for at least 24 hours' notice when possible. We understand that illness, transportation issues, and emergencies happen — just let us know and we'll find you a new time quickly.

Treatment & Services

What we do and how we treat wounds

We offer a comprehensive range of advanced wound care treatments, including:

  • Advanced Debridement — removal of dead or infected tissue (sharp, enzymatic, autolytic)
  • Advanced & Specialty Dressings — foam, hydrocolloid, alginate, silver-impregnated, and more
  • Biological Skin Grafts — advanced tissue products for complex or non-healing wounds
  • Compression Therapy — for venous leg ulcers and edema management
  • Offloading — pressure relief for diabetic foot wounds
  • Infection Management — topical antimicrobials, wound cultures, coordination with prescribing physicians
  • Nutrition & Vascular Coordination — referrals and collaboration with other specialists as needed

Visit our Services page for full details on each treatment.

Debridement is the removal of dead, damaged, or infected tissue from a wound. It is one of the most important steps in wound care because dead tissue prevents healing and harbors bacteria.

We use multiple debridement methods depending on your wound and comfort level:

  • Sharp debridement — precise removal using surgical instruments (numbing is used when needed)
  • Enzymatic debridement — topical agents that dissolve dead tissue painlessly over time
  • Autolytic debridement — advanced dressings that use your body's own moisture to clear debris

We always discuss the approach with you first and ensure your comfort is the priority.

You don't need to figure that out — that's our job. Our specialists perform a comprehensive wound assessment at your first visit, evaluating:

  • Wound size, depth, and tissue type
  • Signs of infection or biofilm
  • Circulation and sensation (critical for diabetic wounds)
  • Your overall health, medications, and conditions
  • Nutritional status

Based on this, we develop a specific, personalized treatment plan — and we explain every recommendation clearly before starting anything.

Conditions We Treat

Types of wounds and who we can help

We specialize in chronic, complex, and non-healing wounds, including:

  • Diabetic foot ulcers — the leading cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputation
  • Venous leg ulcers — caused by poor venous circulation, common in the lower leg
  • Arterial ulcers — caused by reduced blood flow due to peripheral artery disease
  • Pressure injuries / bed sores — stages 1 through 4, sacral and heel wounds
  • Post-surgical wounds — dehisced surgical incisions, post-amputation wounds
  • Traumatic wounds — lacerations and injuries that are not healing properly
  • Burns — partial thickness burns and burn wound management
  • Skin tears — common in elderly patients on blood thinners

If you're unsure whether we can help, call us — we'll give you an honest answer. Visit our Conditions page for more detail.

Yes — diabetic foot ulcers are one of our primary specialties. Diabetic foot wounds are the leading cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputations in the United States, but with proper wound care, the vast majority can be healed and amputation can be prevented.

Our team understands the unique challenges of diabetic wound healing — including neuropathy, poor circulation, and impaired immune response — and uses a multi-faceted approach including debridement, offloading, and advanced dressings.

Early intervention is critical. If you have a diabetic foot wound, don't wait — call us at 866-540-8090.

Yes — we treat all stages of pressure injuries, including sacral ulcers, heel wounds, and other pressure points. Our mobile service is especially valuable for pressure injury patients, who are often bedridden or have very limited mobility — we come directly to you.

Treatment includes wound debridement, advanced dressings to promote moist healing, pressure redistribution guidance, and coordination with the patient's care team on repositioning and nutrition.

You should see a wound care specialist if your wound:

  • Has not shown clear improvement in 2–4 weeks with standard care
  • Has been present for 6 weeks or longer (classified as a chronic wound)
  • Shows signs of infection (increasing redness, warmth, swelling, foul odor, drainage)
  • Is deepening, enlarging, or has exposed tissue
  • Is on the foot or leg of a diabetic patient
  • Is causing increasing pain
  • You have diabetes, poor circulation, or other conditions that impair healing

Wound Healing

Understanding the healing process

With proper specialist care, many chronic wounds show measurable improvement within 2–4 weeks of starting treatment. Complete healing timelines vary widely based on:

  • Wound size, depth, and duration
  • Underlying conditions (diabetes, vascular disease, etc.)
  • Patient nutrition and overall health
  • Circulation to the wound area
  • Patient compliance with the care plan

At your first visit, our specialists will give you a realistic expectation for your specific wound — not a generic timeline. What matters most is that healing is progressing each visit.

Modern wound care science firmly establishes that moist wound healing produces significantly better outcomes than letting a wound dry out. Keeping the wound properly moist (not wet or waterlogged):

  • Enables faster cell migration and tissue regeneration
  • Reduces pain during dressing changes
  • Prevents scab formation (which actually slows healing)
  • Reduces scarring
  • Keeps the wound protected from bacteria

This is why advanced wound dressings — which maintain the optimal moisture balance — dramatically outperform simple dry gauze for chronic wounds.

Absolutely — nutrition has a profound impact on wound healing and is one of the most frequently overlooked factors. Key nutrients your body needs to heal:

  • Protein — essential for tissue repair; deficiency is extremely common in chronic wound patients
  • Vitamin C — critical for collagen synthesis (the scaffolding of new tissue)
  • Zinc — supports immune function and cell division
  • Vitamin A — promotes epithelialization (skin closure)
  • Hydration — dehydration impairs virtually every aspect of healing

Poor nutrition can turn an 8-week healing process into 6 months — or prevent healing entirely. Our team always assesses nutritional status as part of your care plan and may recommend dietary changes or supplements.

Signs of wound infection to watch for:

  • Increasing redness, warmth, and swelling (beyond 48–72 hours)
  • Cloudy, yellow, green, or brown drainage (pus)
  • Foul or unusual odor
  • Increasing pain (not decreasing as expected)
  • Red streaking extending from the wound (serious — seek care immediately)
  • Fever or chills
  • The wound is getting larger or deeper despite treatment

If you notice any of these signs, contact us right away at 866-540-8090. Wound infections can progress quickly and become limb- or life-threatening if not treated promptly.

Chronic wounds fail to heal for specific, treatable reasons. The most common causes include:

  • Poor circulation — insufficient blood flow prevents oxygen and nutrients from reaching the wound
  • Infection or biofilm — bacterial biofilm (a protective layer bacteria form) is invisible and prevents healing; standard antibiotics often don't penetrate it
  • Uncontrolled diabetes — high blood sugar impairs immune function and nerve sensation
  • Repeated pressure or trauma — pressure injuries won't heal if pressure isn't relieved
  • Inadequate wound management — wrong dressing type, dressings not changed at the right frequency
  • Malnutrition — insufficient protein, vitamins, or hydration
  • Certain medications — corticosteroids and some others impair healing

A wound care specialist identifies which of these factors are present and systematically addresses each one. Read more: Why Won't My Wound Heal?
