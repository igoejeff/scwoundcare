Why Won't My Wound Heal? Understanding Chronic (Non-Healing) Wounds

March 3, 2025 6 min read Carolina Wound Care Team Aiken, SC & All of South Carolina

If you're staring at a wound that just isn't getting better — maybe it's on your foot, leg, or another spot that won't close no matter what you try — you're not alone. Millions of people across South Carolina and the rest of the country face the same frustrating question every day: Why won't my wound heal?

Living with a chronic wound can be scary, painful, and exhausting. It can keep you from doing the things you love and even affect your overall health. The good news? Understanding what's happening is the first step toward real healing — and at Carolina Wound Care, we specialize in helping patients just like you move forward.

What Makes a Wound "Chronic"?

A normal wound — think a cut, scrape, or surgical incision — usually heals on its own within a few weeks. But a chronic or non-healing wound is different. It's one that shows little or no improvement after 4–6 weeks — sometimes even longer.

Experts define chronic wounds as those that get "stuck" and don't progress through the natural stages of healing. According to leading wound care organizations, an estimated 8.2 million Americans are living with a chronic wound right now. The longer it goes without proper attention, the higher the risk of infection, hospitalization, or more serious complications.

Important to Know

A wound that hasn't shown real improvement after 4–6 weeks is considered chronic. This isn't a failure on your part — it means the wound needs specialized medical attention that goes beyond home care. The sooner you seek help, the better your outcome.

The 4 Stages of Normal Wound Healing (and Where Things Can Go Wrong)

Your body is amazingly designed to heal itself in four predictable stages. Understanding these stages helps explain why some wounds get stuck and what needs to happen to get healing back on track.

  1. Hemostasis — Blood vessels close and a clot forms to stop bleeding. This happens within seconds to minutes of an injury and creates the scaffold on which healing will build.
  2. Inflammation — Your body sends white blood cells to fight bacteria and clean the area. This is why wounds look red, warm, and swollen — it's normal and necessary. In chronic wounds, this phase becomes stuck in a destructive loop.
  3. Proliferation — New tissue, blood vessels, and skin cells grow to fill the wound. You'll see red, bumpy granulation tissue and the wound visibly shrinking. This is the true rebuilding phase.
  4. Remodeling — The new tissue strengthens and the scar matures over weeks to years. The wound has closed, but the area continues to gain strength over time.

Why Chronic Wounds Get Stuck

Chronic wounds typically get stuck in the inflammation phase. Instead of progressing to repair, the wound stays in a state of constant low-grade inflammation — where inflammatory cells actually destroy new tissue instead of building it. This is why the wound appears to stay the same or even worsen despite home care efforts.

Common Reasons Wounds Stop Healing

When healing gets stuck in the inflammation or proliferation stage, there's almost always an underlying reason. The most common culprits include:

  • Poor circulation — Not enough oxygen and nutrients reach the wound site to fuel repair. Common in peripheral arterial disease and venous insufficiency.
  • Diabetes or high blood sugar — Elevated glucose impairs white blood cell function, reduces circulation, and dramatically slows every phase of healing.
  • Pressure — Sustained pressure from staying in one position cuts off blood flow, preventing tissue from receiving the oxygen it needs to heal.
  • Swelling (edema) — Chronic swelling from venous issues increases tissue pressure and reduces oxygen delivery to wound tissue.
  • Infection or bacterial biofilm — A community of bacteria protected by a slime layer can persist in wounds and actively prevent healing — even without obvious signs of infection.
  • Underlying health conditions — Immune disorders, kidney disease, malnutrition, and certain medications all impact the body's healing capacity.

The result? A wound that stays open, painful, and vulnerable — sometimes for months or years without the right intervention.

When Should You Reach Out?

If your wound hasn't shown real improvement in 4–6 weeks — or if you notice any of the following — it's time to get expert help right away:

  • Increased pain, redness, or warmth around the wound
  • Swelling that is getting worse
  • Unusual odor coming from the wound
  • Drainage that is yellow, green, or cloudy
  • The wound appears to be getting larger or deeper
  • You have diabetes and any wound on your foot or ankle
  • You feel feverish or unwell along with a wound
