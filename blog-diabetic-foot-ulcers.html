Diabetic Foot Ulcers: What Every Patient Needs to Know (and How We Help in South Carolina)

March 2025 7 min read Carolina Wound Care Team Aiken & Charleston, SC

If you or someone you love has diabetes and you've noticed a sore on the bottom of a foot, a blister that won't go away, or an area that just feels "off," you may be facing a diabetic foot ulcer. You're not alone — and there is real hope.

Diabetic foot ulcers are one of the most common — and most serious — complications of diabetes. They can feel frightening because they heal slowly and carry risks if left unchecked. The good news? With the right understanding and specialized care, most people heal well and avoid serious complications. At Carolina Wound Care, we help patients across South Carolina every day turn worry into steady progress.

What Is a Diabetic Foot Ulcer?

A diabetic foot ulcer is an open sore or wound that usually develops on the bottom of the foot, but can appear on toes or other pressure points. It happens when high blood sugar over time damages nerves (neuropathy) and blood vessels (poor circulation).

  • Nerve damage means you might not feel pain from a small cut, blister, or pressure — so the injury goes unnoticed.
  • Poor circulation means less oxygen and nutrients reach the area, making healing much slower.

Know the Numbers

Up to 1 in 3 people with diabetes will develop a foot ulcer at some point in their lifetime. These ulcers are responsible for the majority of non-traumatic lower-limb amputations — yet with early, expert attention, most can be healed successfully.

Common Signs and Symptoms

Because of neuropathy, the first sign isn't always pain. Watch for:

  • A red, warm, or swollen spot on your foot
  • Drainage or odor from a sore
  • Thickened or discolored skin (especially calluses)
  • An open wound that doesn't improve after a week or two
  • Tingling, burning, or complete numbness

Even a tiny crack or blister can turn into an ulcer quickly if you can't feel it. That's why daily foot checks are so important.

Why Diabetic Foot Ulcers Are Serious — And Why Early Action Matters

Without specialized care, these ulcers can lead to infection, deeper tissue damage, and, in some cases, amputation. The risk is real, but it's not inevitable. Studies show that when patients get expert wound care early, healing rates improve dramatically and complications drop significantly.

Living with a diabetic foot ulcer can limit your mobility, affect your independence, and take an emotional toll. Many of our patients tell us they felt isolated or scared before coming to us — and relieved once they had a clear plan and a caring team by their side.

Early Care Changes Everything

Chronic wounds treated by specialists within the first 4 weeks have dramatically better healing rates than wounds treated after months of delay. If you're reading this and wondering whether to call — please call today.

Simple Steps You Can Take Right Now

While professional care is key, these everyday actions make a big difference:

  • Check your feet every day (use a mirror if needed)
  • Keep blood sugar in target range as much as possible
  • Wear well-fitting shoes and never go barefoot
  • Moisturize dry skin (but not between toes)
  • Never try to trim calluses or corns yourself

Don't Wait — Even a Small Change Warrants a Call

If you have diabetes and notice any change in your feet — even a small one — don't wait. Early care is the best protection against serious complications.
